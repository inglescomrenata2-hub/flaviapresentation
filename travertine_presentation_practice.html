Reference

Key Vocabulary for Your Presentation

Learn and review these terms before practising. They will appear throughout the exercises.

travertine
A type of sedimentary rock formed by the precipitation of calcium carbonate from hot springs or geothermal waters.
"Travertines are widely studied in carbonate reservoir research."
pre-salt layer
A geological formation below a thick layer of salt, where significant oil and gas reservoirs are found in Brazil.
"The Brazilian pre-salt layer holds some of the largest reserves in the world."
carbonate rock
A type of sedimentary rock composed mainly of carbonate minerals such as calcite or dolomite.
"Limestone and travertine are both examples of carbonate rocks."
conceptual model
A theoretical framework that explains how a geological system works or how a rock formation was created.
"Several conceptual models have been proposed for travertine formation."
precipitation (geology)
The process by which dissolved minerals crystallise and settle out of a fluid, forming rock layers.
"Travertines are formed through the precipitation of CaCO₃."
reservoir
A porous rock formation that stores oil, gas, or water underground.
"Travertines can act as natural reservoirs due to their porosity."
porosity / permeability
Porosity = the amount of empty space in a rock; Permeability = how easily fluids move through it.
"High porosity does not always mean high permeability."
analogue (geological)
A surface or accessible geological feature that is similar to a subsurface formation and used as a reference.
"Roman travertines are used as analogues for pre-salt formations."
diagenesis
The physical and chemical changes that occur in sediment after deposition and during lithification.
"Diagenetic processes can alter the original porosity of travertines."
hot spring / geothermal fluid
Heated water that rises from underground, often carrying dissolved minerals that form travertine deposits.
"Travertines in Tivoli (Rome) were formed around geothermal springs."

Presentation Phrase Bank

Use these phrases in your speaking exercises. They are the building blocks of academic oral presentations.

Opening & introducing yourself

Good morning/afternoon, everyone. My name is ___ and I'm a geologist from Brazil. Today I'd like to present my research on… The focus of this study is… This presentation covers three main points…

Defining & explaining concepts

Travertines are a type of… In simple terms, this means… Unlike limestone, travertine… The key difference between X and Y is… What distinguishes travertines from other carbonates is… This can be defined as…

Presenting data & findings

Our results show that… As you can see in this slide… The data suggests that… This figure illustrates… We observed that… It is worth noting that… Interestingly, we found…

Comparing & contrasting

Compared to other carbonate rocks… Roman travertines are the most studied analogue… The pre-salt travertines are more complex than… While X tends to…, Y is more likely to… In contrast to limestone… The most significant difference is…

Signposting (moving between sections)

Now I'd like to move on to… Let me now explain the conceptual model… As I mentioned earlier… This brings me to my next point… To summarise this section… Before I continue, let me clarify…

Concluding

To conclude… In summary, this study demonstrates… The main takeaway from this research is… These findings contribute to our understanding of… Further research is needed to… Thank you for your attention. I'm happy to take questions.

Speaking Practice

Presentation Speaking Blocks

Each block covers a different part of your presentation. Press START, speak for the full time without stopping. Write notes first if needed.

01
Opening — 60 seconds
Introduce yourself and your presentation. Say your name, your institution, and the topic. Tell the audience what the three main sections of your talk will be and why this research matters.
💡 Tip: Make eye contact with the audience from the first sentence. Don't read from your slides.

Defining travertines — 90 seconds
Explain what travertines are to an international audience. Define them clearly, explain how they form, and explain the difference between travertines and other carbonate rocks like limestone. Use at least one comparison.
💡 Tip: Start with a simple definition, then add detail. Use "In simple terms…" and "What distinguishes them is…"
Travertines are a type of…Unlike limestone…The key difference is…They form when…

03
Roman travertines as analogues — 90 seconds
Explain why Roman travertines are the most studied occurrence in the world and why they are important for your research. What makes them a good analogue for the Brazilian pre-salt? Compare them to the pre-salt travertines — what is similar and what is different?
💡 Tip: Use comparatives and superlatives: "Roman travertines are the most studied… / They are more accessible than…"
The most well-known…They serve as analogues for…Compared to…One key similarity is…However, they differ in…

04
Conceptual models — 90 seconds
Explain the conceptual models your research proposes for travertine formation in the pre-salt. What are the main ideas? Why are these models important for understanding the reservoirs? What makes your model different from or better than previous models?
💡 Tip: Use: "Our model proposes that… / Based on our data… / This is significant because…"
Our model proposes…Based on our data…This suggests that…Previous studies assumed…However, our findings show…

05
Conclusion — 60 seconds
Summarise the main findings of your research. What are the 2–3 most important takeaways? What does your work contribute to the field? What are the next steps or future research directions?
💡 Tip: End with a strong, clear sentence. Don't introduce new ideas in the conclusion.
In summary…This study demonstrates…The main contribution is…Further research is needed to…Thank you for your attention.

Q&A Preparation

Likely Q&A Questions — Prepare Your Answers

These are the types of questions an American academic audience might ask after your talk. Write notes for each, then practise answering out loud in 30–60 seconds.

How do pre-salt travertines in Brazil differ from the ones found in Rome? Are they a good analogue?
💡 Mention: formation environment, accessibility, diagenesis, similarities and key differences.
What is the main practical application of this research? Could these travertines become significant oil reservoirs?
💡 Mention: porosity, permeability, reservoir potential, exploration importance.
Can you explain more about the conceptual models you proposed? What makes your model different from existing ones?
💡 Mention: what the model proposes, what data supports it, how it differs from earlier models.
What are the main limitations of your study?
💡 It's important to acknowledge limitations honestly. Mention: sample size, access to subsurface data, resolution of methods.
What are the next steps for this research?
💡 Mention future plans confidently. Use: "We plan to… / The next phase will focus on… / We hope to…"
I didn't quite catch your point about [X]. Could you clarify?
💡 Practise rephrasing. Use: "Of course. What I meant was… / Let me rephrase that… / In other words…"
